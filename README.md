# free-order-prophet-inequalities

Prophet inequalities offer a mathematical framework for understanding the limits of decision-making when future information is unknown. A common approach to quantify this uncertainty is to compare the performance of an online decision-maker (gambler) against that of an omniscient prophet who observes all future values in advance. The classical result establishes that a gambler can guarantee at least half of the prophet's expected reward. Stronger guarantees exist for when random variables are drawn from the independent and identical distributions (*iid*) — however, this assumption may not always be realistic in practice.

Previous analyses of prophet inequalities in the independent but non identical (non-*iid*) setting focus on deriving a general competitive ratio for the problem instance. In this thesis, we characterize lower bounds (worst-case competitive ratios) for small non-*iid* instances. Specifically, we prove a tight lower bound of 0.80 for the two-distribution case and provide structural evidence toward a tight conjectured bound of 0.80 for the three-distribution case. These results advance our understanding of how performance guarantees degrade upon relaxing the assumptions of distributional homogeneity, and establish a foundation for resolving the free-order non-*iid* free-order problem for a general $n$-distributions.
